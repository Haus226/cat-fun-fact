Explanation: Why was this collision so strange? In 2022, to develop Earth-saving technology, NASA deliberately crashed the DART spacecraft into the asteroid moonlet Dimorphos. The hope was that this collision would alter the trajectory of Dimorphos around its parent asteroid Didymos and so demonstrate that similar collisions could, in theory, save the Earth from being hit by (other) hazardous asteroids. But analyses of new results show that the effects of the collision are different than expected -- and we are trying to understand why. Featured here is the time lapse video taken by the ejected LICIACube camera LUKE showing about 250 seconds of the expanding debris field of Dimorphos after the collision, with un-impacted Didymos passing in the foreground. In 2026, Europe's Hera mission will reach the asteroids and release three spacecraft to better study the matter.

üåç How to Rotate Crops

That‚Äôs a key question for many farmers, and new research may make it easier to develop growing season plans. [Read more]

ü§ñ The study analyzed large-scale real-world effects of crop rotation using satellite observations and machine learning, revealing that rotating crops often enhances yields but outcomes vary by sequence and environmental factors. In the U.S., corn and soybeans or soybeans and spring wheat rotations increased yields by ~8% compared to monocultures, while soybeans followed by winter wheat reduced yields by ~12%, likely due to delayed planting. Corn after soybeans showed only a 2.5% gain, attributed to reduced fertilizer use as soybeans add nitrogen naturally. Rainier regions benefited more from rotation due to heightened pest/weed pressure, whereas dry areas favored wheat monocultures owing to superior snow retention by wheat residue. Soybean yields rose significantly under warm conditions when rotated, yet preceded by soybeans, corn or wheat yielded less in warm climates because soybeans fix nitrogen less efficiently when hot, and corn/wheat require less nitrogen there. Researchers confirmed practical value of crop rotation for pest management and fertility but emphasized regional nuances necessitating local expertise. The study utilized Landsat and Cropland Data Layer, contrasting with controlled trials to reflect real agricultural decision-making. US farmers predominantly rotate crops, though 18%, 14%, and 6% of corn, wheat, and soybeans respectively remain in continuous cultivation.
